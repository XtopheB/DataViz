\begin{abstract}
    Data visualization is a key element for informing a wide range of users of Official Statistics, from policymakers and media to analysts and statisticians, and to the general public. In this field of communication, National Statistical Offices (NSOs), which compete with other public and private information channels, often distinguish themselves through the quality, accuracy, and efficiency of the information they disseminate, particularly via statistically based graphics, graphical interfaces, and infographics. As with any statistic, these graphics are designed to support the understanding of complex phenomena, enable fair comparisons, and communicate unambiguous facts. While the essence of statistical graphics is to provide visual statistical tests revealing clear patterns and data-based “truths,” some fail to achieve this objective.
    
    This paper aims to highlight commonly encountered “visual lies”, defined by Tufte \citep{Tufte2001} as graphics in which what is represented (or perceived) does not accurately reflect the underlying data. Such representations can distort interpretation and alter the statistical messages conveyed. Our conceptual approach is based on nine rules that illustrate how the understanding of information can be substantially modified through graphical design choices. 
    
    Our objective is not to promote cynicism or suggest that all misleading graphics are intentional, but rather to encourage a critical perspective on data visualization. Examining how visualizations can mislead also provides insight into how they function, how they are interpreted, and how they can be improved to support trustworthy communication of Official Statistics.\\
    
    \textbf{\texttt{Keywords}}: Data Visualization, Exploratory Analytics, Trust,  Infographics, Communication.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
In a world saturated with data and competing narratives, the role of National Statistical Offices (NSOs) as trusted providers of accurate and impartial information is more vital than ever \citep{Hossein2023-DataGov, Schreyer2024-Intw, Perucci22024-Trust, Pullinger2020-Trust}. Increasingly, and more so since the early hours of the Covid-19 pandemic, data visualization has emerged as a critical and powerful medium for the communication of statistical indicators and data-based analysis. When thoughtfully designed, graphical representations enable users - \emph{ e.g.}  policymakers, journalists, or the general public - to grasp complex trends, identify disparities, and draw data-based conclusions. However, as with all forms of communication, graphics carry choices that can undermine the credibility of the information they are meant to convey. This is well documented in statistics were statistical fallacies \citep{Huff1954}, misconceptions and paradoxes \citep{blyth1973simpson} are well-known and taught in statistical courses. 

Data visualization, however, remains an undervalued component of statistical practice, with traditional approaches often prioritizing modeling over communication and perception \citep{Wickham2014}. While basic graphical tools are typically introduced for exploratory analysis, most mainstream statistical training overlooks the cognitive pitfalls and perceptual biases inherent in visual representation \citep{Cleveland1984, Cairo2019, healey2007perception}.


%While most NSOs adhere to principles of objectivity and professional standards, they face growing pressure to compete in a crowded information ecosystem. Social media, data journalism, and private think tanks all vie for public attention—often with more visually engaging or simplified messages. This environment risks incentivizing shortcuts in visual design that may sacrifice nuance, fairness, or even accuracy.

Through a conceptual approach, based on nine "rules", this paper critically examines how visual representations can inadvertently (or purposefully) mislead, drawing attention to what we call “\textit{visual lies}”, a term defined  \citep{Tufte2001} as situations where the representation or the impression conveyed by a graphic does not match the underlying data. While the term "lie"  is deliberately provocative - even if inspired by two seminal references in statistics \citep{Huff1954} and in maps \citep{Monmonier1991} - our aim is constructive: to expose common pitfalls in data visualization and offer best practices that reinforce the credibility and communicative power of official statistics.

While some authors rightfully claim that the choice of any graphical form shouldn't be dictated by rules \citep{Cairo2019}, but should be data, message and audience driven, we have opted here for nine core rules that highlight typical misrepresentation. Here, we present provocative examples, promoting a more thoughtful use of graphics in official statistics, departing from usual practices, habits and software defaults. By identifying the mechanisms through which visual distortions occur and how they can be avoided, we hope to contribute to ongoing efforts within the statistical community to improve user engagement as well as  statistical and visual literacy \citep{sabbati2022Literacy} and, ultimately, reinforce trust in the dissemination of Official Statistics by NSOs.

\section{Data visualization}

Data visualization can be broadly defined as the graphical representation of qualitative or quantitative data to enhance understanding and communication \citep{Cleveland1993Book}. It serves both exploratory and explanatory purposes, helping analysts understand data and enabling audiences to grasp findings quickly and intuitively. One can also consider data visualization as a tool for detecting (discovering) structural information in a given data set \citep{buja2009}. This idea of data visualization as a visual statistical test is presented in  Figure \ref{fig:VisualTest} with an example of how a  simple scatterplot can act as a feature detection.

More precisely, the generic visual test can be formalized as:
\begin{center}
  $H_0$ : \{There is "nothing"  \} = \{\textbf{No} feature, \textbf{No} discovery\} \\
 \emph{vs}\\
  $H_1$ : \{ There is "something" \} = \{There is \textbf{``some''} feature \}
\end{center}

The null hypothesis $H_0$ tested is obviously quite generic and should be, at least implicitly, adapted to  the type of graphic and the data set under scrutiny, but there is a clear parallelism between statistical and visual testing. When analysing and visualizing a data set, any data analysts or statistician implicitly performs this visual test to discover "\textit{what one would never expected to see}" to quote Tukey \citep{tukey1977exploratory}. Although this approach seems grounded in theoretical statistics, this is also true for any viewer, not aware of the power of statistics, looking at any graphic and trying to decipher a "feature" in the graphic proposed. 

In his seminal book, Bertin \citep{Bertin1983English}  has a related pragmatic approach to data visualization, stating that anyone, when looking at any graphic, should get "right answers  to legitimate questions",  provided that it is well designed and accompanied with contextual information (caption, title, etc.).\footnote{While often used interchangeably, \emph{graphics} generally refer to any visual representation of information, including diagrams, icons, and illustrative elements. \emph{Data visualization}, by contrast, specifically involves the graphical representation of quantitative or qualitative information based on data. See \citep{Friendly2008} and \citep{Cairo2012} for historical and conceptual distinctions.}

This outcome - \textit{i.e.} the right answer to a legitimate question - is somehow similar to the result of a test and rejection or not of the null hypothesis $H_0$.


However, despite its simplicity, practicality and effectiveness, this test crucially relies on the good alignment of the visual representation with the data set and the features to be discovered. As for any  statistical test, it carries risks of false detection ("Type I" error), or  failure to detect ("Type II" error) due to graphical distortion or due to a bad choice of the graphical form. Thus, the  relevance and power of the test heavily relies on a strict adherence to good and thoughtful practices and to a good understanding of the drawbacks of visual representations as 2-D objects in multidimensional spaces. 
 
 To measure the extend of a graphical distortion, we use the concept of the “\emph{lie factor}”, defined  as the ratio of the effect shown in a graphic to the effect actually present in the data \citep{Tufte2001}. 
 \begin{equation}
 \nonumber
\text{Lie Factor} = \frac{\text{Size of effect shown in graphic}}{\text{Size of effect in data}}
\end{equation}
 
 A lie factor far from 1.0 indicates a misrepresentation of the data, impeaching any valid visual test.  The risks emphasized here are particularly consequential in the realm of Official Statistics, where public trust and informed decision-making depend on clarity, transparency, and fairness in communication. Institutions \textit{e.g.} the United Nations, OECD, Eurostat  \citep{UNSD2024StoryTelling, OECD2017, Eurostat2022} and some NSOs \citep{ONS2020} have recently produced guidelines on data visualization to ensure that statistical messages are correctly interpreted trough efficient storytelling based on a  careful selection of data visualization.

\section{Nine ``Rules'' for Lying With Graphics}

In the context of official statistics, the cost of visual distortion is particularly high. NSO publications are expected to adhere to strict standards of neutrality, transparency, and interpretability \citep{FPOS1994}. Yet, even subtle design choices, like axis scaling, chart types, or color encodings, can unintentionally introduce bias or ambiguity, (mis)leading the reader to a conclusion that is not one supported by the data  \citep{Cairo2019}. This is boosted by the fact that many statistical producers, together with  the great majority of readers of statistical reports, receive limited training in data visualization \citep{Radermacher2021Literacy, damouras2021}. With the development of ready-to-use  and interactive statistical software, statisticians are also inclined to rely on software defaults and stylistic conventions when using advanced and fancy data visualizations without fully considering the effects of these choices on users perception and understanding.

This section introduces nine categories of visual practices that exhibit serious risks of misleading audiences. These "rules" are drawn from both empirical studies, experiments and recurring issues observed in real-world statistical dissemination. By highlighting these pitfalls, we advocate for a more deliberate and informed approach to graphical design—one that treats data visualization as an integral part of statistical production, not a "decorative afterthought" \citep{cairo2016truthful}.

\subsection*{Rule \#1: Modify Axes}

One of the most common techniques for a misleading representation of a quantity over different categories, is the truncation of the vertical axis, especially in bar charts. As highlighted in Figure \ref{fig:truncated1}  when the baseline does not start at zero, differences between values appear exaggerated, distorting the viewer’s perception of the magnitude of change \citep{Tufte2001,cairo2016truthful}. This issue is especially problematic with bar charts, where bar length encodes value directly and dominates perception, even when the precise value is clearly stated on the axis or in labels. Thus, truncating the axis violates the proportional relationship between data and its visual representation \citep{Leo2019economist}.

While truncating the vertical axis is widely recognized as problematic, less attention is often paid to distortions introduced on the horizontal axis. In time series, discontinuities in the horizontal axis, such as omitting time periods or inconsistently spacing intervals can significantly alter the perceived trend or suggest artificial breaks in continuity. These distortions may occur unintentionally, for instance when data is missing for some periods, or when visualization software (such as Microsoft Excel) automatically spaces data points evenly regardless of their actual temporal values.
Whether vertical or horizontal, such disruptions compromise the temporal or quantitative integrity of the data and must be clearly signaled to the user to preserve trust.

 Since there is no clear guidance on a universal solution to this issue, NSOs should apply the same critical scrutiny to data visualization as they do to written analysis. The implication of unthought or by-default choices carry real consequences since artificially compressing a chart, or zooming in or out around data,   can lead readers to underestimate the severity of a recession or misjudge the urgency of a public policy issue or falsely convince of a strong correlation. Final graphics must be both data- and human-informed and not just shaped by software defaults.\footnote{For example, within the statistical software \texttt{R}, base graphics were traditionally rendered in square boxes, whereas modern graphics produced with \texttt{ggplot()} follow the Grammar of Graphics \citep{wilkinson1999} (hence the \textit{“gg”} in \texttt{ggplot}) and allow for flexible, rectangular formats.}  Sometimes, layout conventions imposed by publisher, such as full-page or two-column formats, introduce constraints on data visualization. These seemingly benign choices can significantly distort how users perceive trends and differences.\\

 \noindent \textbf{ Takeaways:} Practitioners should rarely use of non-standard framing such as long or wide boxing.  For scatterplots, a growing number of visualization guidelines explicitly recommend \textit{banking to 45°}, \textit{i.e.}, choosing an aspect ratio that centers median line slopes around 45°, facilitating more accurate slope perception \citep{Sonning2023Perception}. 
 

\subsection*{Rule \#3: Use Dual-Axis}

Dual‑axis (or two‑scale) charts plot two variables on the same horizontal axis but use separate left and right vertical scales. This allows representing in a single graphic, the evolution of  two variables with different units - hence the two scales -  that are incomparable by nature. These popular graphics often select both scales to align the curves, creating the illusion of a correlation even when the variables are unrelated. Such spurious correlations result from the  freedom of choice in both scales and often goes unnoticed by readers as recalled in \citep{Vigen2015}.

In Figure \ref{fig:dual-axis}, we borrowed a brilliant demonstration  \citep{muth2018} with six charts using the same time-series data and six different dual-axis scaling strategies.

One can easily see how different the  perception of the relationships is between the two curves in the six graphics  and how \textit{spurious correlations} can emerge purely from axis choices in dual-axis charts.\\

 Empirical research has shown that users find dual‑axis charts increase cognitive load and are  more difficult to interpret accurately \citep{Isenberg2011}. As an alternative, some authors \citep{few2008dual, muth2018} suggest to plot indexed values,  converting each variable to a percentage change based on a reference year. This would standardize the two variables to a common scale, reestablishing  the role of the initial comparison of two trends for two variables with same unit. In the case of time series, an interesting alternative  would be  to use a connected scatterplot, where time is eliminated with each variable being on one axis \citep{Evergreen2020Dual}.\\


 \noindent \textbf{ Takeaways:}  In general, it is not recommended to use a single plot to compare values that do not share the same units or magnitudes. Using double axis for comparing time-varying variables in one frame is therefore not recommended in general as the resulting graphic is likely to be too complex and prone to misinterpretation. Some  authors recommend aligning multiple plots (side-by-side or bottom and top) as they are easier to understand \citep{Leo2019economist}, while others try to explain the Dos and Don'ts of creating dual axis charts \citep{Ellis2016Dual-axis}. 


\subsection*{Rule \#4: Select Your Scope}

Choosing the range or scope of a visualization is a powerful editorial decision that can be controversial. Truncating time series, limiting geographic scope, or narrowing axis ranges can subtly or dramatically alter the narrative derived from data, leading to distorted interpretations \citep{Cairo2019}. This practice, often linked to \textit{cherry-picking}, occurs when time series are deliberately truncated or start from an "\textit{interesting}" date, exaggerating growth or decline in the evolution of an indicator. We illustrate this point in Figure \ref{fig:exchangerate} with the  GDP to USD exchange rate during the Brexit negotiations between the UK and the EU in 2017.

In scatter plots, selecting ranges can hide outliers or falsely strengthen correlations. Similarly, in thematic maps, showing only some countries or regions without providing a global context may lead to biased geographic perceptions of a global phenomenon  \citep{bahoken2018English}.

This issue is especially critical in the context of official statistics and Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), where selective framing may appear due a lack of data, inadvertently overstating or understating progress in specific areas, or for a specific time range.\\

\noindent \textbf{ Takeaways:} Scope limitations are choices and are always necessary due to data availability or policy focus. These choices should be justified and clearly indicated in the legend or caption in full transparency.  For NSOs reporting on global indicators, it is essential to maintain consistent baselines and full scopes, unless justified by data and context, and to prioritize a general-to-specific approach in any narrative. 

\subsection*{Rule \#5: Use Pie Charts}

Pie charts are widely used in official statistics, as well as in many publications, due to their apparent simplicity and familiarity among the general public, enabling readers to perceive proportions-to-the-whole with clarity and minimum cognitive efforts \citep{cairo2016truthful}. However, pie charts effectiveness is highly contingent upon the underlying data distribution  that is unknown before visualization is attempted.

While pie charts can effectively show part-to-whole relationships in cases where there are only a few, clearly dominant categories, they become difficult to interpret as the number of segments increases \citep{few2007Pies}, as highlighted in Figure \ref{fig:multiplecatpie} . The perceptual limitations of humans in judging angles make it challenging to accurately compare slices that differ only slightly in size \citep{Cleveland1984}. Furthermore, unlike bar charts, pie charts lack a common baseline for direct comparison, a crucial feature in data visualization that is possible with stacked bar charts \citep{wong2013}.


 Given these limitations,  producers of official statistics are encouraged to use less pie charts and more bar charts (either stacked or dodged) especially when the goal is to support accurate comparisons of shares \citep{Schwabish2014}. More broadly, this example highlights the importance of critically reassessing the suitability of a chosen graphical form once it is applied to actual data. \\

\noindent \textbf{ Takeaways:} Even though pie charts are among the oldest and most widely used graphical forms \citep{Friendly2008}, they remain difficult to compare across categories (see the question in Figure~\ref{fig:multiplecatpie}) and are frequently applied with an excessive number of categories or without sufficient critical review after construction. Because the underlying distribution of categories in a dataset is not known a priori, it may be necessary to adapt the visualization by aggregating certain categories (Figure~\ref{fig:multiplecatpie}a$\rightarrow$ \ref{fig:multiplecatpie}b) or by switching to an alternative chart type, such as bar charts, either stacked or dodged, to preserve clarity and interpretability (Figures~\ref{fig:multiplecatpie}c–~\ref{fig:multiplecatpie}d).

\subsection*{Rule \#6: Cross The Lines}

Plotting several lines that intersect frequently often results in visual clutter, where no clear pattern emerges. Such designs can obscure rather than reveal trends \citep{Schwabish2014}, especially when the lines vary greatly in scale or direction as in the left pane of Figure~\ref{fig:spaghetti}.

Plotting a graphic with real data invariably reveals unexpected features that may trigger the selection of alternatives visualizations, as part of a necessary critical reassessment of the graphic's readability, interpretability and perception. 

\emph{Small multiples} (right pane of Figure~\ref{fig:spaghetti})  consist of a series of coordinated plots presented with consistent axes and layout, where each subplot highlights a single series while the others are shown in gray for reference. In such designs, each panel emphasizes one series, typically highlighted in a saturated or darker color,  while displaying other series in light gray as background references. This draws on principles of \textit{preattentive} processing \citep{Treisman1985}, where visual features such as color and line weight are perceived effortlessly and instantly by the viewer \citep{Ware2012}. 

Small multiples not only reduce cognitive load, but also respect the principle of separation of information layers with the main trend represented by the bold or colored line, while other comparable lines appear in light gray, providing context without distraction \citep{Tufte2006}. They are particularly useful in comparing sub-national regions, demographic groups, or disaggregated indicators in SDG reporting, where clarity and fairness are crucial.\\

\noindent \textbf{ Takeaways:} It is good practice to critically review any graphic once produced, with particular attention to its clarity and interpretability. The use of a checklist addressing design choices, readability, and the choices of visual encodings, can provide a practical and effective framework for such evaluation \citep{Gordon2014}. Considering alternative options such as changing the type of graphic or investigating the use of small-multiples or alternative designs is always recommended.\footnote{With some software, the design of a graphic can be changed dramatically at very low cost by adding a single option or line of code. For example, in \textit{R} with \textit{ggplot}, switching from vertical to horizontal charts is achieved using \texttt{+coord\_flip()}, while creating small multiples for different categories simply requires adding \texttt{+facet\_wrap()} to the code.} 



\subsection*{Rule \#7: Use Radar Plots}

Although not frequently encountered, radar charts (also known as \textit{spider} or \textit{web} charts) are sometimes used in official publications to compare multiple variables across a limited set of entities, such as cities or regions.  They are appealing because they promise an intuitive, holistic view of the overall performance across several dimensions. 

One of the main issues with radar charts is their extreme sensitivity to the ordering of axes.  In Figure~\ref{fig:radar} we represent the same information on the hypothetic performances of 2 regions (North and South) on 5 indicators (A-E) but with axes B and D permuted on the right panel. In the left panel, the polygonal surface for the South Region appears considerably larger than in the right panel, which illustrates the sensitivity of the radar plot to the axes order and highlights the challenges for fair comparisons between North and South relative performances with such graphics.

It has been shown \citep{Bertin1983English} that visual perception of data is strongly influenced by the order in which elements are presented. This is a critical issue here because  radar plot  are precisely used to interpret areas as a proxy for overall performance and radar shapes used for comparisons of entities while these representation are highly unreliable \citep{Bontemps2017, holtz2020radar}. 

This issue is particularly critical in Official Statistics and SDG reporting, where multiple goals or targets are tracked across countries or regions. With radar plots, the perceived differences can be exaggerated or obscured depending on the order in which variables are presented, often dictated by alphabetical or software defaults with no questioning on its impact on the resulting interpretations.\\

\noindent \textbf{ Takeaways:} It is suggested to use alternatives to radar plots for visualizing observations in many dimensions. One may prefer using parallel coordinate plots \citep{holtzPCP} or several graphics (small multiples) for each dimension. If a global performance index is needed, it is preferable to compute and visualize it explicitly rather than relying on the surface of a radar plot, which can be misleading.

\subsection*{Rule \#8: Use Maps}
By design, maps distort reality, simply because to represent a three-dimensional, complex world on a flat surface, they must simplify, exaggerate, and omit some important features of the data. Inevitably, symbols are outsized, details are suppressed, and choices have to be made to represent statistics spatially. We know that it is not only easy to lie with maps, it is, in a sense, unavoidable \citep{Monmonier1991}. While these "white lies" are necessary to make maps readable and useful, some maps can also mislead in more consequential ways, in particular in the realm of statistical maps that use all the visual encoding described previously, and thus concentrate all the problems hindering fair visual tests.

In Figure \ref{fig:MapsSDG551}, we propose four different maps, in an attempt to visualize the spatial distribution of the \textit{Proportion of seats held by women in  national parliaments} (SDG 5.5.1) in Asia-Pacific in 2019. While some common features can be identified, we argue that none of these maps provide answers to legitimate questions, nor help the reader to process a visual test in an attempt to compare the values of this indicator across countries.

Finally, an additional bar chart supports precise ranking and value reading \citep{Munzner2014}. These combined design choices enhance comprehension, especially for non-specialist audiences, and foster data-informed dialogue.\\ 


\noindent \textbf{ Takeaways:} We encourage map makers, especially those involved in the communication of SDG indicators and official statistics, to remain vigilant and question default design settings and to avoid maps produced through reflexive habits \citep{kraak2018SDG}. While maps are often the automatic option when geospatial data is involved, they are not always the most effective way to convey meaning. The decision to use a map should follow a clear purpose, such as the identification and visual testing of spatial trends or regional disparities. When such spatial insights are not the focus, other graphical forms may provide more clarity, precision, and interpretability. 


\subsection*{Rule \#9: Use color}

Distortions and visual lies in data visualization do not only arise from axes or scaling choices, or from misuse or misunderstanding of specific graphics with specific data sets;  they can also result from subtler design decisions. Color, for instance, is a powerful visual cue but carries cultural, gender and perceptual biases. In a striking example \citep{bahoken2018English}, it has been shown that a map with proportional symbols  bearing red circles over black background (Figure \ref{fig:MapsSyrians}, left) can convey a frightening message as compared to a more neutral blue map with green circles (Figure \ref{fig:MapsSyrians}, right), both graphics being technically correct.

These choices are not purely aesthetic but convey strong signals that can alter the perception of the message in an irreversible way. The same color can also have very different meanings across cultures \citep{mccandless2012information}. Yellow may signify health in China but illness in Japan or Hindu contexts. This can potentially affect the perception of any yellow item for international audiences. 

 To capture public interest, and create engaging stories, infographics and the use of icons are  often used. These appealing visuals are  particularly effective, distilling complex analyses into accessible images that quickly convey key messages  to audiences with limited time or technical expertise \citep{page2021DTVCovid}. However, here again, the use of icons and pictograms introduces further risks, and threats to   graphical integrity when symbols are enlarged in proportion to the value they represent \citep{Tufte2001}. Also, these icons, together with their colors may reinforce stereotypes on, \textit{e.g.}, gender, age, wealth or specific locations \citep{ UNVolunteers2020, or2013data}.

 Accessibility adds another layer of complexity since approximately 8\% of men and 0.5\% of women have color vision deficiencies, making some color palettes unusable unless adapted with color-blind safe schemes and redundant encoding \citep{muth2018color}.\\  
 
 \noindent \textbf{ Takeaways:}  Following the Leave-No-One-Behind principles, one should ensure that everyone, regardless of ability, gender, or cultural background, can interpret graphics accurately and without bias \citep{Schwabish2022}. Inclusive visualization  goes beyond color to encompass gender-neutral language, representation choices, and culturally sensitive design principles. For official statistics, where neutrality and trust are essential, applying these principles is not optional but  fundamental features for any communication.

\section{ Discussion: How \textit{Not} to Lie with Graphics}

Just like  texts,  data visualizations are authored documents using a specific language and a semiology \citep{Bertin1983English}. Most of the time, humans use language with good intentions; sometimes, with less precision and a poor grammar or vocabulary leading to either an obscure or oversimplified message. Occasionally, a specific language is used on purpose to reflect a bias or an ideology. In the field of Official Statistics, where trust and neutrality are fundamental, the language of data visualization is just as important as the language we use in writing.\\

It is therefore essential to acknowledge that even official statistical publications may unintentionally mislead through inappropriate selection of the graphic design (the "language"), incorrect choices of visual elements (the "vocabulary")  and  wrong or misleading use of data visualization rules (the "grammar"). Exploring how visualizations can go wrong is not merely a critique, it also offers practical guidelines to how graphics work, how they are read and perceived, why they matter, and how they can be improved or used differently.
Equally important, users must develop the ability to read visualizations critically, understanding both what is shown and what may be unintentionally hidden. Correspondingly, producers of Official Statistics should design graphics deliberately as analytical tools, rather than decoratively \citep{Tufte2006}.\\

As noted recently \citep{sabbati2022Literacy}, data visualization readers should be able to build a story from the data visualization they see, ask the right questions and get a correct answer from the publication they see. The key component - the graphic itself - should be designed thoughtfully to truly convey the intended message for the intended audience,  being data-literate or not. A data-literate audience is obviously more likely to question the narrative than to misinterpret it, but, as we have highlighted here, some design choices, some default options - associated with a lack of awareness of graphics caveats - may result in a misleading interpretation, even among the most visually-literate of us. \\

This highlights  the importance of training in the fields of data visualization, visual literacy and in critical thinking in both producing and consuming visualizations that are are \underline{not} \textit{``lying''}.

\section{ Conclusion}

A lot has been learned on the design of statistical information since the "old" - yet still strikingly relevant -  manual by Brinton in 1914 \citep{Brinton1914}. But despite an abundant literature, very few articles have illustrated the dangers of popular, easy-to-produce graphics that populate official statistics publication and SDG reports. As for statistics, traps, paradoxes and caveats are numerous and are not limited to the  examples presented here.\\

This paper proposes a conceptual framework structured around nine “rules” to identify and prevent  \textit{``visual lies''} defined as situations in which graphical representations distort, obscure, exaggerate or diminish the reader's perception of the underlying data. Through the identification of these "\textit{lies}", our objective is to make visible the mechanisms through which misrepresentation arises, whether unintentionally or deliberately. Several concrete messages emerge from this analysis: 
\begin{itemize}
    \item First, graphical choices must be intentional and justified. Default settings and aesthetic priorities often lead to unnecessary decorative embellishments, inappropriate graphic forms, or distorted proportions. Visual encoding choices must be made deliberately by a human, guided by a commitment to the integrity of official statistics, rather than relying on default, automatic, or AI-driven cosmetic additions. 
    
    \item Second, the creator of any graphic should be guided by three core elements: the analytical question, the structure and content of the data, and the intended audience. Not all spatial data require maps; not all comparisons require bar charts; and not all trends require line graphs. Selecting the appropriate graphical form is itself an act of statistical reasoning.

    \item Third, pedagogical principles such as clarity, coherence, and integrity should be systematically applied. Axes, scales, graphical forms, classifications, colour schemes, and annotations must facilitate critical questioning and support understanding of the message. The consistent application of these principles in graphical design strengthens interpretability and protects against misinterpretation.

    \item Fourth, graphics intended for policy makers and the public must balance accessibility with precision. Simplification may enhance readability, but it must not compromise the necessary statistical complexity of the underlying data, its interrelationships, or its limitations. The ultimate objective of both graphics and statistics is to explain the world in all its dimensions and not to present a simplified, unrealistic, or idealized version of it. Nor should it misrepresent it. Graphics and statistics should then work hand in hand to represent reality as faithfully as possible and to confront the reader's assumptions with evidence.

    \item Fifth, a graphic should rarely stand alone. Contextual information, clear titles, annotations, sources, and accompanying text, are essential to ensure accurate interpretation of what is shown and what is not. Moreover, a single graphic is often insufficient to capture the multiple dimensions of complex phenomena. A set of complementary graphics, such as small multiples, can provide a more faithful and comprehensive representation than a single overloaded or excessively condensed visualization. 
\end{itemize}


For NSOs, these principles carry concrete operational implications. Investing in data visualization capacity is not merely a communication upgrade; it is a safeguard for statistical credibility. Establishing internal guidelines \citep{wong2013}, strengthening visual literacy among both producers and users, and integrating visualization review processes into dissemination workflows can substantially reduce the risk of visual distortion. \\
%In doing so, NSOs reinforce their role as trusted and impartial providers of evidence.\\

In an era of information overload and declining institutional trust, the responsibility of official statistics extends beyond producing accurate numbers and statistics; It includes ensuring that those numbers are represented faithfully. Improving graphical integrity therefore contributes to more than better-looking charts. It strengthens statistical literacy \citep{sabbati2022Literacy}, enhances user engagement, and supports informed public debate. \\

By encouraging deliberate, reflective, and responsible visualization practices, this paper contributes to a culture in which graphics illuminate rather than mislead, and where trust in official statistics is reinforced through robust visualization standards, sustained training, and continuous evaluation of dissemination practices.\\

With the rapid rise of generative AI tools, we can expect a deluge of AI-generated graphics in the near future. Some may have a great influence on public audience or decision makers. Some will definitively use Official Statistics as primary source and others will combine, remix and reuse existing deceptive or misleading graphics scraped from a multitude of websites. This makes it more crucial than ever to promote the importance of high-quality, trustworthy graphics, designed and thought by skilled humans who understand both the principles and the pitfalls of data visualization.

\subsection*{The Misleading Nature of Surface-Based Comparisons}

A common pitfall in data visualization is the use of surfaces — such as circles, squares, or other geometric shapes — to represent quantitative values. Although visually appealing, surface-based encodings are prone to misinterpretation because human perception is ill-equipped to accurately compare areas \citep{Cleveland1984,Spence1990}. This has serious implications in official statistics, where misleading visual impressions can affect public understanding and trust.

For example, in population statistics or economic indicators, using proportional symbols on maps (e.g., bubble maps) or scaled pictograms to represent magnitude can lead to systematic underestimation or overestimation of differences. 

\citet{Huang2016} found that viewers consistently struggle to compare the area of non-aligned shapes, leading to poor estimations, particularly in thematic cartography. Moreover, \citet{Tufte2001} warns against "chartjunk" that emphasizes aesthetic appeal over data integrity — a common occurrence when surfaces are used for storytelling instead of analysis.

One frequent case is the misuse of pictographs or icon-based charts in sustainable development reports to represent statistics such as literacy rates or unemployment, where each icon's size or number suggests a quantity. Without a consistent baseline or scale, such visuals can distort the viewer’s interpretation of the magnitude.

Given these risks, official statistics producers are encouraged to favor position-based encodings (e.g., bar charts or dot plots), which enable more accurate and consistent comparisons. When surfaces must be used (e.g., in proportional symbol maps), visual literacy and careful design (including legends, scale cues, and perceptual testing) become essential.

\subsubsection*{Cartographic Illusions in Official Statistics}

Maps are seductive. They convey authority, suggest objectivity, and offer a compelling sense of completeness. Yet, they are among the most complex graphical objects—and among the most prone to manipulation, intentional or not. As famously stated by \citet{Monmonier1991}, *“maps lie.”* And they do so convincingly.

Statistical maps compress spatial, statistical, and perceptual dimensions into a single view. They mobilize all visual encoding elements—position, area, color, texture—making them especially vulnerable to misleading design choices. This makes fair visual testing nearly impossible without careful scrutiny.

As \citet{Tufte2006} would argue, maps often conflate multiple layers of information without clarifying which layer is foreground and which should remain contextual. The principle of separating information layers is frequently ignored, leading to cluttered and cognitively overwhelming graphics.

In official statistics—especially in SDG reporting—choropleth maps are used extensively to display geographic disparities. But unequal area sizes (e.g., comparing Russia with Luxembourg) distort visual perception of values. Choropleth maps encode data using color intensity within predefined geographic boundaries, but this assumes that the area is a meaningful unit for visual comparison, which is often not the case. As \citet{Cairo2013} reminds us, readers tend to overinterpret larger areas and miss subtler but important variations in smaller ones.

An alternative approach is the **proportional symbol map**, where data values are represented by symbols (typically circles or bubbles) placed at specific locations. These allow data to be detached from area size, making it easier to represent quantities more directly. According to \citet{Slocum2009}, symbol maps can be more effective when the goal is to convey absolute quantities rather than densities or rates. They are also more flexible when displaying multiple variables through symbol shape, size, or color.

However, symbol maps are not without problems. As \citet{Cleveland1984} notes, humans are notoriously poor at judging area size—especially circles. Overplotting in dense urban areas and lack of clear spatial boundaries can further reduce legibility. Moreover, the location of symbols often matters: placing them at administrative centroids can mislead when population or activity is clustered elsewhere.

In short, neither map type is inherently superior. The choice must depend on the variable of interest (rate vs. count), spatial granularity, and the visual task at hand. For official statistics, where both fairness and clarity are critical, the decision between a choropleth and a symbol map should be treated as part of the statistical reasoning—not left to default templates in mapping software.

Perhaps most critically, maps invite readers to assume they are seeing “everything.” Yet what’s excluded—a region, an indicator, or a time period—matters just as much . As such, statistical mapping must be approached with the same critical thinking that statisticians apply to modeling assumptions: What is included? What is excluded? Who chose the projection? Why this legend? \citep{Houtum2020MapsArrows}.

Maps are not neutral containers of data. They are arguments and as such, producers of official statistics must scrutinize them with the same rigor they apply to any other form of evidence.
