\begin{abstract}

\emph{Data visualization is one of the key elements to inform a wide range of statistics consumers, from medias to politicians and obviously,  the general public. In this field of communication,  NSOs who are in competition with other public and private communication channels, must differentiate themselves by the quality, accuracy and efficiency of the information they release, in particular through statistically-based graphics and graphical interfaces. But as for any statistics, data visualization should be designed to help the understanding of complex phenomena and  facilitate fair comparisons. They must act as visual statistical tests  and reveal unambiguous patterns and data-based “truths”. This paper propose to highlighting commonly encountered “visual lies”, that is data visualizations where what is represented (or perceived) is not what is present in the data set. Such representations can alter the perception of the underlying statistical messages and values delivered to various users and medias. Our approach is based on 10 rules that can be used to drastically change the understanding of the information represented from any given data set. We preach here counterexamples to reveal the best practices, in an attempt to promote data visualizations as a powerful tool for trustworthy communication of Official Statistics.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
In a world saturated with data and competing narratives, the role of National Statistical Offices (NSOs) as trusted providers of accurate and impartial information is more vital than ever \cite{Hossein2023-DataGov, Schreyer2024-Intw, Perucci22024-Trust, Pullinger2020-Trust}. Increasingly, and since the early hours of the Covid-19 pandemic, data visualization has emerged as a critical and powerful medium for public communication of statistical indicators and data-based analysis. When thoughtfully designed, graphical representations enable users, \emph{ e.g.}  policymakers, journalists, or general public, to grasp complex trends, identify disparities, and draw data-based conclusions. However, as with all forms of statistical communication, graphics carry choices that can enhance or undermine the credibility of the information they are meant to convey. This is well documented in statistics were statistical fallacies \cite{Huff1954} and paradoxes \cite{blyth1973simpson} are well-known and well taught in 101 statistical courses. 

Data visualization, however, remains an undervalued component of statistical practice, with traditional approaches often prioritizing modeling over communication \cite{Wickham2014}. While basic graphical tools are typically introduced for exploratory analysis, most mainstream statistical training overlooks the cognitive pitfalls and perceptual biases inherent in visual representation \cite{Cleveland1984}, \cite{Cairo2019} \cite{healey2007perception}.


%While most NSOs adhere to principles of objectivity and professional standards, they face growing pressure to compete in a crowded information ecosystem. Social media, data journalism, and private think tanks all vie for public attention—often with more visually engaging or simplified messages. This environment risks incentivizing shortcuts in visual design that may sacrifice nuance, fairness, or even accuracy.

This paper critically examines how visual representations can inadvertently (or purposefully) mislead, drawing attention to what we call “visual lies”, a term defined by \cite{Tufte2001} as situations where the representation or the impression conveyed by a graphic does not match the underlying data. While the term "lie"  is deliberately provocative even if inspired by two seminal references in statistics \cite{Huff1954} and geospatial representation  \cite{Monmonier1991}, our aim is constructive: to expose common pitfalls in data visualization and offer best practices that reinforce the credibility and communicative power of official statistics.

While some authors rightfully claim that the choice of any graphical form shouldn't be dictated by rules \cite{Cairo2019}, but should be data, message and audience driven, we have opted here for ten core rules that highlight typical misrepresentation. Thus, we present here counterexamples to promote a more thoughtful use of graphics, departing from usual practices, habits and software defaults. By identifying the mechanisms through which visual distortion occurs and how it can be avoided, we hope to contribute to ongoing efforts within the statistical community to improve user engagement, statistical and visual literacy and ultimately trust in the dissemination of public data by NSOs.


\section{Data visualization}

Data visualization, broadly defined as the graphical representation of qualitative or quantitative data to enhance understanding and communication \cite{Cleveland1993Book}, serves both exploratory and explanatory purposes, helping analysts understand data and enabling audiences to grasp findings quickly and intuitively. Following \cite{buja2009}, we can also consider consider data visualization as a  tool for exploring data and  detecting (discovering) structural information in a given data set. This idea of data visualization as a visual statistical test is presented in  Figure \ref{fig:VisualTest} with an example of how a  simple scatterplot can act as a feature detection.

\begin{figure}[tbh]
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width = 0.22\linewidth]{RandomPoints.pdf}
            \includegraphics[width = 0.22\linewidth]{LinearPoints1.pdf}
            \includegraphics[width = 0.22\linewidth]{UshapedPoints.pdf}
            \includegraphics[width = 0.22\linewidth]{GroupsPoints.pdf}

\caption{Simple scatter plot can serve as a visual test. Only in the left hand-side plot $H_0$ is \textbf{not} rejected ("nothing" detected). In all three others, there is a feature (correlation, U-Shape, two groups) detected. }
%- "\emph{There's nothing}" (plot \#1), $H_0$ rejected - "\emph{There's a trend}" ( plot \#2), $H_0$  rejected - "\emph{There's a U-shaped relation}" (plot \#3), $H_0$ rejected - "\emph{There's groups}" (plot \#4)    }
\label{fig:VisualTest}
\end{center}
\end{figure}

More precisely, the generic visual test can be formalized as:
\begin{center}
  $H_0$ : \{There is "nothing"  \} = \{\textbf{No} feature, \textbf{No} discovery\} \\
 \emph{vs}\\
  $H_1$ : \{ There is "something" \} = \{There is \textbf{``some''} feature \}
\end{center}

The implicit null hypothesis $H_0$ tested can obviously vary and should be specific to  the type of graphic and the data set under scrutiny, but there is a clear parallelism between quantitative testing and visual testing. When analysing and visualizing a data set, any data analysts or statistician implicitly perform this visual test to discover "\textit{what one would never expected to see}" to quote J. Tuckey \cite{tukey1977exploratory}. This is also true for any viewer, not aware of the power of statistics,  when looking at any graphic. 

In his seminal book,  \cite{Bertin1983English}  has a related but pragmatic approach to data visualization stating that anyone, when looking at any graphic, should get right answers  to legitimate questions  provide that it is well designed and accompanied with contextual information (caption, title, etc.). This outcome, \textit{i.e.} the right answer to a legitimate question, is somehow similar to the result of a test and rejection or not of the null hypothesis.

However, despite its simplicity, practicality and effectiveness, this test crucially relies on the good alignment of the visual representation with the data set and its features under scrutiny. As any statistical summary, it carries risks of false detection (Type I error), or not detecting an existing (in the dataset) feature (Type II error) due to graphical distortion or due to a bad choice of the graphical form. Thus, the test relevance and power heavily relies on a strict adherence to good practices and to a good understanding of the drawbacks of visual representations as a 2-D object in a multidimensional space. 
 
 To measure the extend of a graphical distortion \cite{Tufte2001} introduced the concept of the “\emph{lie factor}”, defined as the ratio of the effect shown in a graphic to the effect actually present in the data. 
 \begin{equation}
 \nonumber
\text{Lie Factor} = \frac{\text{Size of effect shown in graphic}}{\text{Size of effect in data}}
\end{equation}
 
 A lie factor far from 1.0 indicates a misrepresentation of the data, impeaching any valid visual test.  The risks emphasized here are particularly consequential in the realm of Official Statistics, where public trust and informed decision-making depend on clarity, transparency, and fairness in communication. This why institutions like Eurostat and OECD  \cite{Eurostat2022, OECD2017} ar some NSOs \cite{ONS2020} have recently emphasized the increasing importance of effective visualization in ensuring statistical messages are not only seen but also correctly interpreted.

\section{Ten Rules for Lying With Graphics}

In the context of official statistics, the cost of visual distortion is particularly high. NSO publications are expected to adhere to strict standards of neutrality, transparency, and interpretability \cite{FPOS1994}. Yet, even subtle design choices, like axis scaling, chart types, or visual or color encodings, can unintentionally introduce bias or ambiguity, (mis)leading the reader to a conclusion that is not one supported by the data  \cite{Cairo2019}. This is compounded by the fact that many statistical producers, together with  the great majority of readers of statistical reports, receive limited training in data visualization \cite{Radermacher2021Literacy, damouras2021}. With the development of ready-to-use software and interactive statistical software,  statisticians are also inclined to  rely on software defaults or classical stylistic conventions without fully considering their communicative effects.

This section introduces ten common visual practices that risk misleading audiences. These "rules" are drawn from both empirical studies and recurring issues observed in real-world statistical dissemination. By highlighting these pitfalls, we advocate for a more deliberate and informed approach to graphical design—one that treats data visualization as an integral part of statistical production, not a "decorative afterthought" \cite{cairo2016truthful}.

\subsection*{Rule \#1: Modify Axes}

One of the most common and well-documented techniques for misleading with graphics is the truncation of the vertical axis, especially in bar charts. As highlighted in Figure \ref{fig:truncated1}  when the baseline does not start at zero, differences between values appear exaggerated, distorting the viewer’s perception of the magnitude of change \cite{Tufte2001,cairo2016truthful}. This issue is particularly critical for bar charts, where the length of the bar directly encodes the value. Truncating the axis violates the proportional relationship between data and its visual representation \cite{Leo2019economist}.


\begin{figure}[tbh]
\includegraphics[width = 0.45\linewidth]{Truncated_Bar_Graph.png} \includegraphics[width = 0.45\linewidth]{Bar_graph.png}
\caption{Same data represented with and without vertical axis truncation. A bar char with an axis starting at an ad-hoc value exaggerate differences (left), while there are small absolute variations in the data set (right). Source: \cite{wiki:TruncatedBar} }
\label{fig:truncated1}  % <- label must be after caption
\end{figure}

While truncating the vertical axis is widely recognized as problematic, less attention is often paid to distortions introduced on the horizontal axis. In time series, discontinuities in the horizontal axis, such as omitting time periods or inconsistently spacing intervals can significantly alter the perceived trend or suggest artificial breaks in continuity. These distortions may occur unintentionally, for instance when data is missing for certain periods, or when visualization software (such as Microsoft Excel) automatically spaces data points evenly regardless of their actual temporal values.
Whether vertical or horizontal, such disruptions compromise the temporal or quantitative integrity of the data and must be clearly signaled to the user to preserve trust.

Interestingly,  \cite{Correll2019} experimentally showed that this exaggerating effect is also found in line charts (Figure \ref{fig:truncated2}). This poses the question of when it is appropriate or not to start line chart at the zero origin, in particular when the origin is out of the range of the data set.\footnote{ One typical example is the representation of earth temperature over time. Starting the vertical axis (temperature) at zero degree Fahrenheit makes no sense and annihilate the visual representation of the recent and dramatic increase in temperature due to global warming.} 

\begin{figure}[tbh]
    \centering
   \includegraphics[width = 0.4\linewidth]{ExperienceTruncatingAxis-Bars-Correll2019.JPG} \includegraphics[width = 0.4\linewidth]{ExperienceTruncatingAxis-Lines-Correll2019.JPG}
\caption{Truncation serves to exaggerate effect sizes for  both  bar and line charts. }
    \label{fig:truncated2}
\end{figure}

While bar charts remain one of the most widely used visualization types in official statistical publications due to their familiarity and simplicity, \cite{Xiong2021-VisuBar} demonstrate that comparing values across multiple grouped or separated bar charts introduces significant perceptual challenges. Their study shows that users struggle to make accurate comparisons when bars are not presented within a unified coordinate system, leading to increased cognitive load and potential misinterpretation.

\subsection*{Rule \#2: Modify the Framing Design}

Framing a graphic in a wide or tall format can also affect the perception of a phenomena \cite{Pandey2015Deceptive}, typically for time series,  widely used in Official Statistics for \textit{e.g.} economic indicators, public health trends, or environmental measures.

Framing a time series in a wide or tall rectangular plot can significantly affect how viewers perceive trends, rates of change, and volatility. Compressing or stretching the time axis alters the slope angles of line segments, thereby biasing interpretation. In Figure \ref{fig:aspect-ratio-comparison}, for example, wide formats tend to flatten trends, while tall formats exaggerate variations.

\subsubsection*{There is more...}

Not only the framing itself has to be controlled and adjusted according to the data, but the level of zoom and the space left around data points can subtly affect the perception of some important features. In a seminal work  \cite{Cleveland1982} showed how our perception of correlation is affected by this zooming effect, as highlighted in Figure \ref{fig:correlation}. 

\begin{figure}[tbh]
\centering
 \includegraphics[width =0.6\linewidth]{ScalesScatterplotConcentrationCleveland.JPG}
 \caption{The correlation between $Xs$ and $Ys$ is the same in the two graphics, while experiment shows that reader perceive a higher correlation in the left graphic \cite{Cleveland1982}   }
 \label{fig:correlation}
\end{figure}

In the context of official statistics publications, the implication of unthought or by-default choices carry real consequences since artificially compressing a chart, or zooming in or out around data,   can lead readers to underestimate the severity of a recession or misjudge the urgency of a public policy issue or falsely convince of a strong correlation.

Given the high stakes of public policy decisions, NSOs should adopt design principle and approach data visualizations with the same critical scrutiny applied to  written statistical analysis. The final design of any published graphic should be both human- and data-driven, not merely the result of software defaults\footnote{For instance, in \texttt{R}, base plots are typically rendered in square plotting windows by default.} or the arbitrary layout conventions of publishers, such as full-page or two-column formats. These seemingly benign choices can significantly distort how trends and differences are perceived by end users.

\subsection*{Rule \#3: Use Dual-Axis}

Dual‑axis (or two‑scale) charts plot two variables on the same horizontal axis but use separate left and right vertical scales. This allows representing in a single graphic, the evolution of  two variables with different units - hence the two scales -  that are incomparable by nature.  These popular graphics often select both scales to align the curves, creating the illusion of a correlation even when the variables are unrelated. Such spurious correlations result from the  freedom of choice in both scales and often goes unnoticed by readers as recalled in \cite{Vigen2015}.

In Figure \ref{fig:dual-axis}, we borrowed a brilliant demonstration from \cite{muth2018} with six charts using the same time-series data but different dual-axis scaling strategies, to showcase how different is the  perception of the relationships between the two curves in the six graphics  and how \textit{spurious correlations} can emerge purely from axis choices.

\begin{figure}[tbh]
    \centering
\includegraphics[width = 0.8\linewidth]{LCRost-dualaxisCrazy.png}
\caption{Modifying left and right axes leads to very different perceptions of the relationships between two curves.  (source: \cite{muth2018})}
\label{fig:dual-axis}
\end{figure}

Empirical research has shown that users find dual‑axis charts increase cognitive load and are  more difficult to interpret accurately \cite{Isenberg2011}. Authors recommend aligning multiple plots (side-by-side or bottom and top) as they are easier to understand \citep{Leo2019economist}, while \cite{Ellis2016Dual-axis}  explains the Do’s and Don’ts of creating dual axis charts. As an alternative \cite{few2008dual} and \cite{muth2018} suggest to plot indexed values converting each variable to a percentage change based on a reference year. This would standardize the two variables to a common scale, reestablishing  the role of the initial comparison of two trends for two variables with same unit. In the case of time series, an interesting alternative  would be  to use a connected scatterplot, where time is eliminated with each variable being on one axis \cite{Evergreen2020Dual}.

\subsection*{Rule \#4: Select Your Scope}

Choosing the range or scope of a visualization is a powerful editorial decision. Truncating time series, limiting geographic scope, or narrowing axis ranges can subtly or dramatically alter the narrative derived from data, leading to distorted interpretations \citep{Cairo2019}.  This practice, sometimes associated to  \textit{Cherry-picking},  is likely to occur in time series with a period starting from an "\textit{interesting}" date can exaggerate apparent growth or decline in the evolution of any indicator. We illustrate this point in Figure \ref{fig:exchangerate} with the  GDP to USD exchange rate during the Brexit negotiations between the UK and the EU in 2017.  

\begin{figure}
\includegraphics[width = 0.45\linewidth]{GBP-USDRate-Brexiters-Zoom.jpg} \hspace{0.55cm}\includegraphics[width = 0.45\linewidth]{GBP-USDRate-5Y-Annotated.jpg}
\caption{The GBP to USD exchange rate appears increasing (left)  when cleverly "picked"  (by Brexit partisans) within a global declining period (right)}
\label{fig:exchangerate}
\end{figure}
 
In scatter plots, omitting certain ranges can hide outliers or falsely strengthen correlations. Similarly, in thematic maps, showing only certain countries or regions without providing a global context may lead to biased geographic perceptions of a global phenomenon as illustrated in \cite{bahoken2018English}.

This issue is especially critical in the context of Official Statistics and Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), where selective framing may appear due a lack of data, inadvertently overstating or understating progress in specific areas, or for a specific time range. 

Scope limitations are choices and are always necessary due to data availability or policy focus. These choices should be justified and clearly indicated in the legend or caption in full transparency.  For NSOs reporting on global indicators, it is essential to maintain consistent baselines and full scopes, unless justified by data and context and to prioritize a general-to-specific approach in any narrative. 

\subsection{Rule \#5: Use Pie Charts}

Pie charts are widely used in official statistics, as well as in many publication, due to their apparent simplicity and familiarity among the general public, enabling readers to perceive proportions with clarity and minimum cognitive effort \citep{cairo2016truthful}. However, pie charts effectiveness is highly contingent upon the underlying data distribution  that is unknown before visualization is attempted. 

\begin{figure}[tbh]
\includegraphics[width = 0.9\linewidth]{Compare3Pies.png}
\caption{Comparing pie charts can be a difficult visual test: In which graphic(s) the category "b" (light blue)  is greater that category "c" (pink)? }
\label{fig:PieCharts}
\end{figure}

In Figure \ref{fig:PieCharts}, for example,  even an experienced reader may find difficult to identify the largest sector in each chart and, will find extremely difficult to tell with confidence in which graphic(s) the category "b" (light blue)  is greater than category "d" (gray).\footnote{The answer is provided in Figure \ref{fig:PieChartsBars} at the end of the paper. }

The perceptual limitations of humans in judging angles make it challenging to accurately compare slices that differ only slightly in size \citep{Cleveland1984}. Furthermore, unlike bar charts, pie charts lack a common baseline for direct comparison, a crucial feature in data visualization \citep{wong2013}.

While pie charts can effectively show part-to-whole relationships in cases where there are only a few, clearly dominant categories, they become difficult to interpret as the number of segments increases as highlighted in Figure \ref{fig:multiplepie}.

\begin{figure}[tbh]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{Pie11Categories.png} 
\hspace{0.55cm}
\includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{Pie4Categories.png} 
\caption{Pie charts with multiple categories become cluttered and difficult to visualize (left) while grouping categories can help (right) the shares may still be difficult to compare. }
\label{fig:multiplepie}
\end{figure}

 Given these drawbacks, official statistics producers should consider using bar charts or dot plots instead, particularly when the priority is to enable precise comparisons.

\subsection*{The Misleading Nature of Surface-Based Comparisons}

A common pitfall in data visualization is the use of surfaces — such as circles, squares, or other geometric shapes — to represent quantitative values. Although visually appealing, surface-based encodings are prone to misinterpretation because human perception is ill-equipped to accurately compare areas \citep{Cleveland1984,Spence1990}. This has serious implications in official statistics, where misleading visual impressions can affect public understanding and trust.

For example, in population statistics or economic indicators, using proportional symbols on maps (e.g., bubble maps) or scaled pictograms to represent magnitude can lead to systematic underestimation or overestimation of differences. 

\citet{Huang2016} found that viewers consistently struggle to compare the area of non-aligned shapes, leading to poor estimations, particularly in thematic cartography. Moreover, \citet{Tufte2001} warns against "chartjunk" that emphasizes aesthetic appeal over data integrity — a common occurrence when surfaces are used for storytelling instead of analysis.

One frequent case is the misuse of pictographs or icon-based charts in sustainable development reports to represent statistics such as literacy rates or unemployment, where each icon's size or number suggests a quantity. Without a consistent baseline or scale, such visuals can distort the viewer’s interpretation of the magnitude.

Given these risks, official statistics producers are encouraged to favor position-based encodings (e.g., bar charts or dot plots), which enable more accurate and consistent comparisons. When surfaces must be used (e.g., in proportional symbol maps), visual literacy and careful design (including legends, scale cues, and perceptual testing) become essential.
